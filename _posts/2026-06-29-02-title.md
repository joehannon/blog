**Tests**

The way to test closures is to solve the equations that use the closures and compare the results with reality (experimental data), other closures and references. As part of my open source species_limits.py project, I included the CVODE integrator from the excellent[^7] [scikit-sundae](https://scikit-sundae.readthedocs.io/en/stable/) library to solve the ODEs for each species. I used the same mixing parameters and calculations as the 2025 paper from Baldyga's group, including the analytical solution for intensity of segregation, allowing the variance of f to be calculated easily at each time (or position).  I also included the ability to perform an epsilon "sweep" from very slow mixing (1.0E-6 W/kg) to very fast mixing (1.0E+6 W/kg) with the same fixed reaction kinetics. That allows exploration of any closure over the full range of mixing timescales from almost completely segregated reaction conditions through to almost completely well-mixed reaction conditions.  We are looking for closures that behave correctly at both ends of this spectrum and especially in the middle where both mixing and kinetics are influential and interact strongly. Each integration proceeds to 99.9% consumption of the limiting reactant; that is A in this case, fed through stream 1 where f=1.  B and C are fed in stream 2 where f=0.  The volume ratio of the streams is 15, so the mean of f is 1/(15+1)=0.0625.

**Baldyga's limits**

Baldyga (1994) uses a closure interpolating between a pair of infinitely fast reaction subsets for both competitive-consecutive and competitive-parallel reactions.  In the competitive-consecutive case, he discusses how $f_{s}$ moves as the product of the first reaction is produced and the second reaction is enabled to proceed. In the 2009 and 2025 papers from his group on the full 5-reaction system, the movement of $f_{s}$ is directly calculated from the amount of R (or S) already produced during reaction and interpolation is between the no-reaction limit and a combination of two infinitely fast reaction limits. Combination is a powerful idea as a means of identifying a unique infinitely fast reaction limit. C(f) for all species can then be obtained by interpolation between that (moving) limit and the no-reaction / mixing line.  I implemented a this method as one closure option in species_limits.py. Full mathematical details of my implementation are available [here](https://joehannon.github.io/blog/2026/07/01/01-title.html).  

**Simulation results**

From the beginning, I tried to keep my implementation general and for example, do not rely on any reaction in any system being infinitely fast. I included an option for the user to stipulate/ hardwire precisely which reaction subsets to combine in the manner of the 2009 and 2025 papers; that enabled me to reproduce the method in the papers exactly, I believe.  (I am in contact with Magdalena Jasińska in Baldyga's group and am checking additional details).  I named the method "blend_fs". I also tried to leave the door open to generalize the published approach beyond systems identical to the azo-coupling example and one idea was to identify reaction subsets that are missing one or more potential products; those subsets could then be weighted by the amount of other (not missing) products they produce, to calculate a moving limit.  (When this specific idea was applied to the 5-reaction systm, it used three limits and did not select one of the limits used in the 2025 paper; that is because limit 31 is missing two products, R and T).

Where two flat lines are visible, the average species concentration lies outside the limits defined by blend_fs and the reaction rate is "clamped" to stay just within the limits; clamping reflects an internal inconsistenty in the cloure under clamped conditions.  Clamping can lead to unexpect changes in the shape of reaction rates that are not physical.

This plot will be compared later with Figure 11 b) in the 2025 publication. They are very similar but not identical and the differences will be discussed. 

Other diagnostics produced from a simulation include the simulation CPU time (blend_fs is generally fast) and the number of times the ODE solution "clamped" C(f) to keep C(f) as used in the reaction rate calculation within known limits, even if the ODE solution average had driften outside those limits.  The consequence of such a clamp is an internal inconsistency in the model, whereby the beta-averaged C(f) no longer equals the average calculated by solving the ODEs.  That can lead to unexpected and likely unphpysical changes in reaction rate during the simulations.  We found that clamps were frequent for blend_fs and will discuss that more later.
