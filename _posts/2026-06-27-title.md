**A long-standing problem**\
Since completing my PhD in 1992 and long before that and a few times since, I have returned to the problem of modeling chemical reactions that are mixing-sensitive.  I had the pleasure to meet and work with John Bourne in the late 1980s and early 1990s and to meet Jerzy Baldyga a few times including in Dublin with Steve Hearn in the late nineties.  Building on early findings by Ed Paul of Merck & Co., Bourne and Baldyga laid a great foundation in the field, developed interesting mathematical models, established the kinetics of liquid-phase test reactions and linked the results of mixing-sensitive reactions with fluid mechanics and energy dissipation rates. The field was developed separately in the combustion world where there was an early emphasis on CFD and closure models dealt primarily with steady-state problems in gases, with less emphasis on chemical kinetics and more on turbulence.

Modeling these systems allows ideas to be tested against experimental data and to establish whether the model and the theory behind it is true.  For good models that leads to intuitions of what variables affect the result and that guides problem-solving and design of experiments to solve problems.  A challenge to going further and predicting real industrially useful system behaviour quantitatively has always been the availability of chemical kinetic parameters; because at least some of the chemistry is fast, special experimental setups are required. Computer power also remains a limiter, especially when dealing with transient systems and using CFD with large computational grids.

In these articles I will discuss some practical methods available, list current gaps where I know them and report on some mostly computational research I have been doing to clarify some details and address some gaps.

The material should be interesting for anyone making fine chemicals or pharmaceutical active ingredients and to the broad community of system modelers and CFD users.
