**Extending the closure from the 2025 paper**

Having developed ray_limit and spent more time than I should researching this field, I realized that the closure presented in the 2025 paper as Equation 41 can be broadened in scope compared to the 2025 paper by combining all three (actually five) reactions into a single stoichiometrically correct summary line, much like ray limit does.

Equation 41 states:

$$f_{s}=\frac{(1+\beta)C_{B0}+C_{C0}}{C_{A0}+(1+\beta)C_{B0}+C_{C0}}$$

That is based on a reaction scheme in Equations 32 and 33 that combines A+B>R and A+R>S with extent 1 mole for the first line and $\beta$ moles for the second, taken together with A+C>Q:

$$(1+\beta) A + B > (1-\beta) R + \beta S$$

$$A + C > Q$$

Equation 32 is not one of the reactions that are included in the ODE solution. In Equation 32, stoichiometric coefficients have been replaced by reaction extents. That is entirely valid when generating a summary of the reactions that have taken place up to a given moment, based on the extents to which the underlying true reactions have proceeded.

The change in molar amount of a species as a result of one or more true reactions is calculated classically from the extents of the true reactions occuring and the stoichiometric coefficients on the species in those true reactions:

$$n_{i}-n_{i0}=\sum_{j}e_{j}\nu_{ij}$$

A broader single-line summary statement of the reactions that have taken place, in which Q formation proceeds to extent $\gamma$ is:

$$(1+\beta+\gamma) A + B + \gamma C > (1-\beta) R + \beta S + \gamma Q$$

That single summary reaction will have a moving $f_{s}$ that differs from the 2025 paper's version of the equation and can in principle summarize a wider range of reaction conditions, including those where the Q-forming reaction does not proceed significantly; generalizing the extent of reaction 1 to $\alpha$:

$$(\alpha+\beta+\gamma) A + \alpha B + \gamma C > (\alpha-\beta) R + \beta S + \gamma Q$$

$$f_{s}=\frac{(\alpha+\beta+\gamma)\min(C_{B0}/\alpha, C_{C0}/\gamma)}{C_{A0}+(\alpha+\beta+\gamma)\min(C_{B0}/\alpha, C_{C0}/\gamma)}$$

With $\alpha=1$ and $\gamma=1$ this becomes substantially equivalent to Equation (41). 

These analytical equations for fs all assume that no reactant is fed through more than one stream; it is rare for that assumption to be violated in fast reaction systems.

I believe that this line of reasoning can be generalized to any set of reactions but have not seen that written anywhere before.  In the more involved case of the 5-reaction system we are using for illustration, additional reactions A+B>T and A+T>S take place.  The full scheme is then: A+B>R, A+R>S, A+B>T, A+T>S, A+C>Q and denoting the extents as $\alpha$, $\beta$, $\gamma$, $\delta$ and $\epsilon$ respectively, the one-line extent summary is: 

$$(\alpha+\beta+\gamma+\delta+\epsilon) A + (\alpha+\gamma) B + \epsilon C > (\alpha-\beta) R + (\gamma-\delta) T + (\beta+\delta) S + \epsilon Q$$

$$f_{s}=\frac{(\alpha+\beta+\gamma+\delta+\epsilon)\min(C_{B0}/(\alpha+\gamma), C_{C0}/\epsilon)}{C_{A0}+(\alpha+\beta+\gamma+\delta+\epsilon)\min(C_{B0}/(\alpha+\gamma), C_{C0}/\epsilon)}$$

With $\alpha=1$, $\gamma=0$, $\delta=0$ and $\epsilon=1$ this becomes substantially equivalent to Equation (41). I have used the above extended closed form equation to calculate fs by hand for a range of values of $\alpha$, $\beta$, $\gamma$, $\delta$ and $\epsilon$ and tabulate below relevant results for the current illustration [full-size](/blog/docs/assets/hand_calcs_fs_extend_2025.png):

![hand calculations of fs](/blog/docs/assets/hand_calcs_fs_extend_2025.png){: width="1000" }

Colour coded formatting shows green for large values and red for small. It is clear that the broader fs formula above leads to a wider range of possible fs values than those shown in the 2025 paper.

The workings in this section of the post may be thought of as an extended version of the 2025 paper's closure and are starting to look strikingly similar to the ray_limit closure I have been using.  Both approaches dynamically collapse the entire reaction system onto a single line summary reaction that is stoichiometrically historically correct at the current time.  You can think of this single line summary as a report on what has happened in the actual reactions up to the current time. Infinitely fast reaction limits can be obtained from the single summary reaction and used to bound the estimates we need of C(f) with the no-reaction limit / mixing line bounding C(f) on the other side. 

The method to obtain the infinitely fast limit is to find the point, $f_{s}$ at which reactants from each stream cannot coexist. ray_limit does this by scaling its ray to its maximum possible extent. An equivalent alternative is to obtain fs using the above formulae.

This proposed extended version of the 2025 paper's approach is not described in the 2025 paper or the prior 2009 paper or anywhere else that I have seen.  Nevertheless, it appears to be quite generally applicable, as any reaction system may be summarily written in this single-line form, replacing stoichiometric coefficients with overall reaction extents for each species.

**Results with extended_2025 closure**

I decided to implement this extended version of the 2025 paper's closure approach in my code. When I started that today, I wanted to access extent of reaction information for each line and then construct each Greek letter term, calculate $f_{s}$ and then the "tent" plots for each species dynamically using the full stoichiometric matrix of the five reactions as the solution proceeded. I realized that I did not need to do all of that work in order to use the extended closure.  The overall extents for each species in the single summary line may be calculated exactly from the amount of each reactant that has disappeared and product that has formed, relative to the mixing line; there is no need to tease out or solve for the individual extents of each reaction line.  It is exactly equivalent to the complex (Greek letters) form oif the reaction equation above to use the ODE solution for the mole balance on each component to determine the coefficients in the overall summary reaction:

$$ (C_{A}-C_{A}^{0}) A + (C_{B}-C_{B}^{0}) B + (C_{C}-C_{C}^{0})  C > (C_{R}-C_{R}^{0}) R + (C_{T}-C_{T}^{0}) T + (C_{S}-C_{S}^{0}) S + (C_{Q}-C_{Q}^{0}) Q $$

It was now obvious to me that this extended version of the C(f) closure in the 2025 paper would require very similar code and give very similar results to my existing ray_limit approach, which also collapses the reaction system dynamically and then finds the infintely fast limit of the overall summary reaction.  I gave the approach its own separate name within the code, "extend_2025".  A key point of difference from ray_limit is that with extend_2025, I am using the analytical formulae above to obtain fs.  (I verified by hand that the coefficients in the summary equation when entered in the fs equations above lead to the same fs as found by ray_limit when it finds the maximum extent of the reaction ray).  For completeness, I wrote a full mathematical summary of extend_2025 [here]().

This journey also made me wonder whether the extended equations given above were in fact the equations used when Figure 11 b) of the 2025 paper was produced.  I will know more about this when I see the code used for the results presented in the 2025 paper. Both ray_limit and the extended 2025 closure equations lead to much better behaviour of the solution than blend_fs, with no clamping and a smooth movement of fs over the whole range of epsilon.  We already know that ray_limit calculations already agree very well with Figure 11 b) from the 2025 paper. Using ray_limit of extend_2025 produces a shift of the XQ and XS versus epsilon curve to the right; that was the main aspect in which my implementation's results differed from those presented in the 2025 paper.
