**How to select the limits to use for closure**

The closure problem for fast reactions between two separate streams arises because we do not know the (product of the) instantaneous concentrations needed to calculate the average reaction rate.  Turbulent fluctuations in concentration, that is the temporal fluctuations in concentration at a fixed location, require estimation/ modeling.  The combustion community and later Baldgya and others use the concept of mixture fraction and its variance to represent the fluctuations.  Mixture fraction is a conserved scalar with value 1.0 in stream 1 and 0.0 in stream 2.  When there is no chemical reaction, the relationship between species concentration (average or instantaneous) and mixture fraction is given by the "mixing line" for each species, shown here for the 5-reaction azo-coupling at typical concentrations - A is fed in stream 1 with B and C fed in stream 2:

![Mixing line when no reaction occurs](/blog/docs/assets/Mixing_line.png){: width="400" }

Turbulent fluctuations in the local value of f may be described statistically using the mean and variance of f and the beta proability density function (pdf) is a good representation that is commonly used.  When the streams first meet, the variance is total, with essentially no contact with molecules from the other stream.  The variance is reduced by mixing on various scales, in a cascade from larger to smaller eddies; as variance reduces, the pdf gets tighter and eventually becomes a delta function at the mean of f, corresponding to a completely mixed system with no concentration fluctuations. The mixing-sensitivity of chemical reactions occurs when the reactions are fast enough to take place at least partually while mixing is still in progress.

For the 5-reaction version of azo-coupling, the reactions are as follows:
```
"reactions": [
  "R1: A + B -> R",
  "R2: A + R -> S",
  "R3: A + B -> T",
  "R4: A + T -> S",
  "R5: A + C -> Q"
  ],
  
  "rate_constants": {
    "R1": 12238,
    "R2": 1.835,
    "R3": 921,
    "R4": 22.25,
    "R5": 124.5
  },
```

The mixing line allows calculation of the number of molecules (or moles) of each substance that would be present at any location if no reaction occurred.  That is one extreme limit that approximates the situation when reaction kinetics are very slow.  There is another extreme that describes behaviour when kinetics are infinitely fast; in that case, reactants from the streams cannot co-exist, as any collision would lead to immediate product formation.
